How to Play Imperial Dawn

Imperial Dawn is a Tabletop Role Playing Game. This means that you will sit around with your friends, with all but one of you playing the role of a particular character in a story that all of you create together. One person will be the Game Master, which means that they will play the rest of the world around your characters, and provide the bulk of challenge and conflict that the story needs.

You will step into the role of a unique character that you yourself will create. You will act through this character from the beginning of their story to the end. Sometimes that story ends in success. Perhaps they seal up a seam in the fabric of the universe, or maybe they find love, or perhaps all they want is just to survive the day. It's also possible that they could be poisoned, eaten, blown to smithereens, and turned into a cloud of singing flies. We live in a strange and harsh world, but The Scrap, the world of Imperial Dawn, can be even harsher and stranger. Every inch of the world is saturated with free flowing magic, and the great power and beauty that brings are balanced by the confusion and terror of the unknown.

I know nothing of the story that you are about to embark upon, except this: You will not be bored.

The Basics

This might look like a lot at first, and in some ways it is. That said, you don't need to know everything. You can get started with just a few ideas, and pick up the rest as you go.
  • You can have your character try anything that you can think of, but they might not succeed at it.
  • Your character has six Traits and some Abilities.
    • Traits are a set of numbers that represent your general capability in six areas. three is bad, four is average, and six is great.
    • Abilities are either continuous bonuses that are always in effect, or specific things that you can reliably do. You can take actions other than your abilities.
  • When you try to do a thing, the GM may have you roll one die for each point you have in your most relevant Trait.
  • When you roll, count one "success" for each die that lands with a three or higher. Dice that get lower values are generally ignored.
  • The more successes the better. It takes two successes to accomplish something moderately difficult. Neither the number of dice rolled, nor the actual numbers on the dice have any impact. Only the number of successes matters.
  • When timing matters, like in combat, you have one major action and one minor action per turn. During these times a few more rules kick in that generally don't matter otherwise.
    • Entering a Stance ability takes a major action, and once you're in it you stay in it until you end it, become Grappled, Helpless, Prone, or unconscious.
    • You can stay in a Stance for up to a minute, then you naturally fall out of it, though you can take another major action to re-enter it immediately.
    • Stances are exhausting and when you exit one, you will take one point of Exhaustion.
    • Active abilities can only be used on your turn, reactions can be used at any time, passives are always in effect.
    • Reactions can be used regardless of turn, but only in response to certain events.
    • Passives are always in effect, so long as you are alive.
    • You can spend an action, major or minor, to move a number of meters up to your Agility. You can use both of your actions this way.
  • If you have Wounds equal to or exceeding twice your Vigor, you're Helpless. Four times your Vigor kills you.
